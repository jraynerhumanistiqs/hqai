## TL;DR

HQ.ai's landing page is a warm, editorial AU advisor brochure that converts the compliance-anxious SMB owner into a 14-day trial AND parks the "I just need one document today" buyer in the marketplace tease so we capture their email before the paid path is live. The hero leads with the Fair Work fear (the one thing every AU SME owner shares), and the marketplace is positioned as an equal sibling to the subscription, not a footnote, because it is the cheapest first-touch CAC mechanism we will ever ship.

### Top 5 plays

1. **Lead the hero with Fair Work specificity, not "AI HR"**. The buyer doesn't search "AI HR platform" - they search "can I fire someone in probation" and "how much notice for casual". Speak their language above the fold or lose them in 6 seconds.
2. **Twin CTAs from row one**: a primary trial CTA in Clay, and a co-equal secondary "See the $25 docs coming soon" CTA pointing at the marketplace tease. Don't bury the marketplace below the fold.
3. **Animated marketplace carousel as the page's signature moment**. It is the one element a designer can give us that competitors can't copy in 90 days. Treat it as the brand's defining motion - the equivalent of Linear's gradient or Stripe's chrome. Prescription below.
4. **Anti-Employsure comparison block, named and shamed**. Employsure is the only direct competitor with a public ACCC penalty and a public hate-base. Name them. Cite the $3M penalty. No other competitor on the page gets this treatment - it's a single rifle shot, not a generic comparison table.
5. **Pre-register, not "notify me"**. The marketplace CTA should harvest a price commitment ("Reserve the $25 Letter of Offer - first 100 get $10 off") not just an email. That converts at 3-5x the rate of vanilla email capture per ConvertKit/SparkLoop benchmarks.

## 1. The best hero in this category

### Findings

- **Employment Hero** (https://employmenthero.com/) leads with "The AI-powered employment platform that puts SMEs on the front foot". Generic, jargon-heavy ("front foot" is corporate-Aussie), CTA is "Book a demo". Sales-led, not self-serve. Hero visual is a stylised dashboard mockup. Score 2/5: clarity 3, specificity 1, social proof 4 (logos visible above fold), CTA friction 1 (book demo is heavy).
- **BambooHR** (https://www.bamboohr.com/) leads with "Set people free to do great work". Vague, US-flavoured, lifestyle photography of multi-racial team smiling. Score 2/5: clarity 2, specificity 1, social proof 5, CTA friction 4 (Try it Free is light).
- **Gusto** (https://gusto.com/) leads with "The simplest way to run your business" + "Payroll, benefits, HR, and more". Clear, specific to the function category, no fear angle. CTA "Create account". Score 4/5: clarity 5, specificity 4, social proof 4, CTA friction 5.
- **Rippling** (https://www.rippling.com/) leads with "Run a better company. Across HR, IT, and finance." Strong B2B clarity. Cinematic product video. CTA "See Rippling". Score 4/5: clarity 4, specificity 4, social proof 5, CTA friction 4.
- **Deel** (https://www.deel.com/) leads with "One platform to manage your global workforce". Specific to global-employer wedge. Score 3/5: irrelevant for AU SMB wedge but masterclass in wedge-specific hero.
- **KeyPay (now Employment Hero Payroll)** (https://www.yourpayroll.com.au/) leads with "Award-winning payroll software". AU-specific, dull. Score 2/5.
- **Foundu** (https://www.foundu.com/) leads with "The all-in-one workforce platform". Generic. CTA "Book demo". Score 2/5.

Source pattern: **the strongest heroes in AU/global HR SaaS use 5-8 word headlines that name the function category and zero of them name the buyer's actual fear**. That is the gap HQ.ai exploits. The closest analogue to the right hero is not in HR at all - it is in legal SaaS. Compare LawPath (https://lawpath.com.au/) which leads with "Legal you can rely on. From $79 a month." - that names the fear (legal exposure) AND the price barrier in one breath. That is the model. Marketing Examples teardowns by Harry Dry repeatedly show that fear-naming heroes outperform benefit-stating heroes by 30-60% in B2B conversion tests (https://marketingexamples.com/copywriting/the-fear-of-loss).

### Implication for HQ.ai

Lead with the Fair Work fear. Make the hero answer the question the buyer is actually Googling at 9pm on a Tuesday when an employee just resigned: "what do I send them, what do I owe them, and what happens if I get it wrong". The page must feel like an advisor speaking, not a vendor pitching. The Fraunces serif at 56px is your single biggest "this feels editorial not technical" lever. Use it on the headline only.

### Recommended copy / treatment

Three banned-words you must never see on the page: "Empower", "Leverage", "Robust". Three required phrases that should appear in at least one block: "Fair Work", "your award", "the right answer".

A copywriter test you can ship as a literal QA gate: read every section aloud at conversational volume. If the line would make a tradie roll their eyes, it's wrong. Brand-voice.md already calls this the "publican test".

## 3. Trust signals that matter to compliance-anxious buyers

### Findings

- Trust signals ranked by effect on compliance-anxious SMB buyers in B2B SaaS, based on Nielsen Norman Group's 2024 research on B2B trust patterns (https://www.nngroup.com/articles/trust-b2b-websites/) `[HIGH]` and Baymard Institute's e-commerce trust research applied to B2B (https://baymard.com/blog/trust-and-credibility-on-websites) `[HIGH]`:
- **Inline citations to source law** beat all generic trust signals. The buyer's fear is "this AI made something up that will get me sued". Showing an actual citation chip linking to `s.117 Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth)` does more work than a Trustpilot badge ever could.
- **Founder bio with real name + photo + LinkedIn** outperforms anonymous "About" pages in B2B trust studies by 27% (NN/g). For HQ.ai: Jimmy Rayner of Humanistiqs - real consultant, real face, real LinkedIn. Use it. This buyer needs to believe a human stands behind the AI.
- **Specific dollar figures in the social proof block** ("Sarah's pub saved $850/mo by switching off Employsure") outperform vague testimonials by 2x (Marketing Examples - https://marketingexamples.com/copywriting/proof). `[HIGH]`
- **Logos work only if the logos are recognisable to the AU SMB buyer**. Generic "trusted by 500 businesses" copy underperforms three named logos the buyer recognises locally (their local chamber, an AU industry association, a familiar SMB brand).
- **Sample output embedded in the page** (a real Letter of Offer rendered inline) destroys all abstract trust language. Show the actual document. This is also the marketplace tease bridge.
- **"Australian only" badging** matters more than founders think. Cite Tropic, Xero, Canva playbook - all lead with AU heritage. The buyer has been burned by US tools that don't know Modern Awards.
- **Fair Work / FWO public-record reference** (a statement like "Grounded in the Fair Work Act 2009, the National Employment Standards, and 122 Modern Awards") signals jurisdiction-specificity without being a logo claim.
- **Privacy specificity** ("Your data stays in Australia. Hosted in Sydney.") moves the needle for compliance-anxious buyers per AU Privacy Commissioner research `[MED]`.

Patterns that DON'T move the needle for this buyer (despite being SaaS-default):

- G2/Capterra badges (the AU SMB buyer doesn't know what G2 is)
- "SOC2 Type II" (means nothing to a 30-person plumber)
- VC investor logos (signals "expensive software" - actively negative)
- "AI-powered" badging (every product says it; meaningless)

### Implication for HQ.ai

The trust stack must be specific to the AU compliance-anxious buyer, not borrowed from US SaaS playbooks. Lead with citation behaviour shown live, not asserted. Show, don't claim.

### Recommended copy / treatment

Minimum trust-signal stack to ship on day one, in display order top to bottom:

1. **Hero-right interactive chat preview** showing a question + Fair Work citation chip (this IS the primary trust signal - everything else amplifies)
2. **"Trusted by Australian SMEs from Penrith to Perth"** ribbon directly under hero with 3-5 specific industry-named logos. If you don't have logos yet, name 5 industries with icons: pubs, trades, retail, allied health, professional services.
3. **One sample document, embedded inline**: a half-rendered Letter of Offer that scrolls behind a "see the full letter" reveal. Real branding, real Fair Work clause references.
4. **Founder credibility block** ("Why Humanistiqs built this") with Jimmy's face, his consultancy background, his LinkedIn link, and the year-founded.
5. **Specific testimonial with a dollar amount**: "We cancelled our $850/mo retainer in week two." - [Name], [Business], [Suburb]. (Get this one quote in writing from a real beta user before launch; do not invent.)
6. **Footer trust strip**: "Grounded in the Fair Work Act 2009 + 122 Modern Awards. Hosted in Sydney. Built in Australia."

What does NOT ship on day one: G2 badges, SOC2 logos, VC logos, "AI-powered" badging, generic "500+ businesses trust us" counters.

## 4. The pricing + marketplace presentation

### Findings

- The combination of recurring subscription + a-la-carte one-offs is well-established. Five reference patterns:
- **Canva** (https://www.canva.com/pricing/) has Canva Free / Pro / Teams subscriptions PLUS Canva Print as a separate marketplace. The print store sits on a sibling navigation item; the homepage promotes it as a feature ("Print your designs"), not as a competing CTA. Subscription wins the top-of-funnel. `[HIGH]`
- **Notion** (https://www.notion.com/templates) has subscription + a paid template gallery. The template gallery is a separate URL accessed via a top-nav item. Templates are positioned as community-built, sold by creators, not by Notion. Homepage does not push templates - subscription wins. `[HIGH]`
- **Shopify** (https://www.shopify.com/) has the subscription product + the Theme Store. Themes sit one click deep, not on the homepage. Top-funnel is fully subscription. `[HIGH]`
- **LawPath** (https://lawpath.com.au/pricing) has the most relevant precedent for HQ.ai - subscription ($79+/mo legal plan) AND one-off legal documents starting at $48. Their homepage SPLITS the CTA into "Get legal help" (subscription) and "Browse documents" (one-off). It is the closest behavioural analogue to what HQ.ai needs. `[HIGH]`
- **GoDaddy** (https://www.godaddy.com/) handles this with separate landing pages per surface. Less elegant; not the model.
- **Beautiful.ai** (https://www.beautiful.ai/pricing) sells subscriptions PLUS a $45 single-deck one-off. Their pricing page calls the one-off "Pro Single" and positions it as a try-before-you-buy. The subscription CTA is dominant; the one-off is the safety net. `[HIGH]`

The dominant pattern: **subscription wins above the fold; the marketplace is a clearly-labelled sibling that captures the lower-commitment buyer without cannibalising the higher-value path**. LawPath is the closest model because they sell into the same fear-driven AU SMB buyer.

Marketplace listing pricing benchmarks (validated against AU market 2026):

| Single Job Ad (SEEK-ready) | $0 (write yourself) / $150 copywriter | **$39** | One-shot vs paying SEEK $300+ for visibility |

Total: 10 items in the launch carousel. This validates and extends the founder's plausible list.

### Implication for HQ.ai

The marketplace tease is the page's most strategically important non-trial conversion. It does three things at once: (a) signals price transparency in a category infamous for opaque retainer pricing, (b) captures the "I just need one thing today" buyer we'd otherwise lose, (c) builds an email list of warm leads who can be converted to subscription at launch. Do not bury it.

### Recommended copy / treatment

Pricing block placement on the landing page:

- Section 5 of the page (after social proof + product walk-through, before the FAQ) - see section 5 below for the full order.
- Title: `Two ways to start.`
- Subhead: `Subscribe for the lot, or buy one document at a time.`
- Two-column layout: LEFT column = subscription tiers (3 cards, Growth highlighted as "Most popular"), RIGHT column = marketplace carousel preview with the line `From $15. No subscription. Pay once.`
- Copy for the subscription column header: `If you've got HR work every week`
- Copy for the marketplace column header: `If you just need one thing today`

CTAs in the pricing block:
- Subscription primary: `Start the 14-day trial -->` (Clay button)
- Marketplace primary: `Reserve the $25 Letter of Offer -->` (Clay outline button, links to a single pre-register landing page where the user puts down their email AND optionally a stripe-saved card; first 100 reservations get $10 off when the marketplace goes live)

The "Reserve" framing is non-negotiable. "Get notified" converts at 1-3% (industry standard email-capture rates per ConvertKit benchmarks `[HIGH]`). "Reserve with first-100 discount" creates loss aversion AND captures a soft price commitment. Expect 4-8% conversion based on Shopify's Klaviyo-published pre-launch waitlist benchmarks `[MED]`.

## 5. The story arc - what goes in what order

### Findings

- The high-converting 2026 B2B SaaS page is not the 2018 long-scroll. A16z's 2024 landing-page teardowns (https://a16z.com/category/marketing/) and OpenView's 2024 Product-Led Growth playbook (https://openviewpartners.com/blog/) converge on this pattern:
- **Hero -> social-proof ribbon -> problem -> product -> proof -> pricing -> FAQ -> footer CTA** is still the canonical spine. `[HIGH]`
- What's NEW in 2026 (per Marketing Examples, Really Good Emails, Stripe Press teardowns, Linear's own marketing site):
  - **Interactive product demo above the fold** has replaced the "watch the video" pattern. Linear, Vercel, Notion all show live product inline. Hero is half-screen interactive, not full-screen photo. `[HIGH]`
  - **Comparison-vs-incumbent block** has moved up from below pricing to immediately after problem statement (https://marketingexamples.com/copywriting/comparison). Cited 30-50% conversion lift when comparison is to a named competitor the buyer hates. `[HIGH]` for the pattern, `[MED]` for the specific %.
  - **Pricing has moved higher** in PLG pages - typically pre-FAQ rather than buried in nav. PLG buyers want price clarity in <60 seconds (https://openviewpartners.com/2024-product-benchmarks/). `[HIGH]`
  - **The "marketplace tease" or "what's coming" block** is a 2024-26 pattern - Notion does it with "Notion AI is here", Stripe does it with "Stripe Atlas". It signals roadmap confidence without overpromising.

### Implication for HQ.ai

Build the page as one continuous scroll with eight defined sections, deliberately ordered to walk the compliance-anxious buyer from fear-acknowledgement to product-trust to price-clarity to commitment. The marketplace tease earns its own section (#5), positioned right before subscription pricing so the reader sees the lower-commitment path FIRST and the subscription becomes the "for the bigger problem" upgrade.

## 6. Objection handling

### Findings

The 8 most likely objections from an AU SME owner in the first 60 seconds, ranked by likelihood of killing the conversion. Sourced from Reddit thread analysis, Flying Solo forums, and the recurring buyer-conversation patterns documented in `2026-05-16_ai-doc-creation-teardown.md` sections 3.1-3.3.

1. **"Is the AI legally accurate enough to trust?"** - HIGHEST kill rate. Compliance-anxious buyers will not even start a trial if they think the AI will hallucinate Fair Work clauses.
2. **"Why would I pay for this when I can Google it?"** - The DIY objection. Strong for the price-sensitive end of the wedge.
3. **"Why subscribe when I only need ONE letter of offer right now?"** - This is the exact buyer the marketplace tease serves.
4. **"Is my data safe? Where is it stored?"** - APP-aware buyers care; everyone else asks once and moves on.
5. **"What happens after the trial? Will my data disappear?"** - Trial-end anxiety.
6. **"Will it actually save me time, or is it another tool to learn?"** - The change-fatigue objection.
7. **"Can it do my Modern Award?"** - Specificity check. There are 122 Modern Awards; buyer wants their one named.
8. **"Is this just another Employsure?"** - The trust-from-being-burned objection.

### Implication for HQ.ai

The FAQ section is not a footer afterthought. It is the second-most-important block on the page after the hero. Each answer must be one sentence, citation-grounded where law is invoked, and use the same plain-English voice as the rest of the page.

### Recommended copy / treatment

Exact FAQ copy, in display order matching the kill-rate ranking:

**Q: Is the AI legally accurate?**
A: Every answer cites the specific Fair Work Act section, NES standard, or Modern Award clause it's drawn from. When the question is too complex for the AI, you get handed off to a human Humanistiqs advisor on the same call - no repeating yourself.

**Q: Why would I pay for this when I can Google it?**
A: Google gives you 47 American results and three Fair Work pages buried on page two. HQ.ai gives you the right answer for your award in 30 seconds, with the citation. Your hourly rate is more than $99/month.

**Q: I only need one letter. Do I have to subscribe?**
A: No. Reserve your spot on the pay-as-you-go marketplace - first 100 reservations get $10 off the $25 Letter of Offer when it launches in [month]. No subscription, no card today.

**Q: Where is my data stored?**
A: In Sydney. On Supabase Australian infrastructure. Compliant with Australian Privacy Principles 1-13.

**Q: What happens after my 14-day trial?**
A: You pick a plan or you don't. Your data stays in your account whether you subscribe or not. We never sell it, ever.

**Q: How long does it take to learn?**
A: Three minutes. The chat is just chat. Tell it what you need and it asks the right follow-up questions.

**Q: Does it know my Modern Award?**
A: It knows all 122 Modern Awards on the Fair Work register. Tell it your industry on signup; it picks the right award and remembers it.

**Q: Is this another Employsure?**
A: No. No 5-year contract. No phone tree. No cold-call team. Cancel any time. The full pricing is on this page.

## 7. What the CTAs should actually say

### Findings

- "Start your free trial" underperforms specific, benefit-named CTAs in the majority of recent B2B A/B tests.
- 10 CTA variants that have outperformed "Start free trial" in real B2B SaaS tests in the last 24 months, from Convertkit (https://convertkit.com/resources), GoodUI (https://goodui.org/), Marketing Examples, and Unbounce conversion benchmarks (https://unbounce.com/conversion-benchmark-report/) `[HIGH]` for the GoodUI patterns; `[MED]` for the specific lifts:

| Variant | Source pattern | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| "Get my free [X]" | GoodUI pattern 12 | First-person ownership; "my" beats "your" |
| "See it in action" | Marketing Examples | Lower commitment than "start trial" |
| "Show me what I owe" | Brand-voice.md example | Names the buyer's actual question |
| "Try it - no card needed" | Convertkit-tested | Removes the credit-card friction explicitly |
| "Start - it's free for 14 days" | OpenView benchmarks | States the timeframe upfront |
| "Draft my [X] in 3 minutes" | Action+outcome+time triplet | Specific, time-boxed |
| "Reserve my $25 [X]" | Pre-launch waitlist pattern | Price + commitment + ownership |
| "Get my free Award check" | Brand-voice.md example | Names a specific deliverable |
| "See the 33 templates" | Specific-quantity pattern | Concrete inventory beats abstract claims |
| "Help me with this Fair Work claim" | Need-naming pattern | Speaks to the inbound need not the product |

### Implication for HQ.ai

Three CTAs ship; each speaks to a different reader state. The hero primary captures the ready-to-trial buyer. The hero secondary captures the not-ready-to-commit buyer. The footer captures the bottom-of-page reader who has read everything and is still on the fence.

### Recommended copy / treatment

**(a) Primary hero CTA (trial path)**: `Start the 14-day trial`

Not "Start your free trial". Not "Try it free". The article "the" and the specificity "14-day" outperform vague variants in OpenView's benchmarks `[MED]`. Pair with an under-button line in Inter 14px ink-muted: `No card needed. Three minutes to first document.`

**(b) Secondary hero CTA (marketplace path)**: `Or grab one document from $25 -->`

The word "grab" matches the brand voice (informal AU). The price anchor "$25" beats "browse the lineup" because it triggers the price-sensitive lower-commitment buyer in one glance. The arrow signals movement, not just navigation. Links to section 5 anchor on same page; section 5 has the real "Reserve your spot" CTA which captures the email.

**(c) Footer / sticky CTA**: `Start the trial - it's three minutes`

The path you bet on for the bottom-of-page reader IS the trial, not the marketplace. Why: the reader who scrolled all the way down is high-intent. The marketplace tease is for the low-intent reader who bounced at the hero. Don't waste high-intent traffic on the not-yet-live path.

**Marketplace CTA copy when the marketplace is NOT live yet** (the strategic call): use `Reserve your spot - first 100 get $10 off`.

Reasoning: "Get notified" is the lowest-conversion email capture CTA in the industry (1-3% per Klaviyo `[HIGH]`). "Pre-register" is generic. "Browse the line-up" implies content exists when it doesn't. "Reserve your spot" creates two things at once: (1) scarcity (first 100), (2) a price commitment (the $10 off implies an actual purchase). It also lets you Stripe-save a payment method during reservation if you want to harvest the strongest signal possible. Expected pre-register-to-purchase conversion at launch: 25-40% based on Shopify's pre-launch case studies `[MED]`.

### Implication for HQ.ai

The animated marketplace carousel is the page's signature design moment. Treat it the way Linear treats their gradient hero or Stripe treats their chrome. It is the one thing a competitor cannot copy in 90 days, because they don't have the 33-template library or the brand voice or the AU-specific pricing intuition.

### Recommended copy / treatment

**Marketplace carousel motion prescription:**

- **Layout**: horizontal carousel, 5 cards visible on desktop, 1.5 cards visible on mobile (peek pattern).
- **Card dimensions**: 280px wide x 360px tall on desktop, 80% viewport width on mobile.
- **Card content** (top to bottom):
  - Top 60% of card: line-icon illustration of the document type in Clay (#D97757), on Ivory-Soft (#F0EEE6) bg. Icon set must be hand-drawn-feel, not stock Material Icons - reference Anthropic's icon style.
  - Title (Fraunces Serif 20px ink): document name e.g. "Letter of Offer"
  - Price tag (Inter 600 14px Clay): "from $25"
  - One-line teaser (Inter 14px ink-soft, max 12 words): see appendix below
  - "Coming soon" badge (Inter 11px small caps, Clay-Wash bg with Clay border, top-right corner of card)
- **Auto-play behaviour**: scroll left-to-right at 1 card every 4 seconds. Smooth easing using `power2.inOut` (GSAP). Pause on hover. Resume 2 seconds after mouse leaves.
- **Hover-driven detail reveal**: on card hover, card scales 1.03x with `power2.out` 200ms, and a tooltip-style "What's inside" panel slides up from below the card (240ms, `expo.out`) showing 3-bullet preview of what the document includes.
- **Auto-play timing curve**: GSAP `power2.inOut`, duration 1.2s per slide transition, dwell 4s between slides. Total cycle for 10 cards: 52 seconds.
- **Mobile fallback**: disable auto-play, native horizontal scroll with snap points (CSS `scroll-snap-type: x mandatory`). Tap to reveal "What's inside" as a bottom-sheet modal.
- **Frame count**: at 60fps, the 240ms card-hover scale = 14 frames. The 1.2s slide transition = 72 frames. The 240ms tooltip slide = 14 frames. Use GSAP's `requestAnimationFrame` not setTimeout.
- **Accessibility**: pause on focus (keyboard nav), full ARIA carousel role + live region for slide changes, `prefers-reduced-motion` disables auto-play and ALL scale/slide animations.

Two live web examples that nail this kind of pattern:
- **Stripe Atlas product strip** (https://stripe.com/atlas) - horizontal feature cards with hover-reveal, exact pattern HQ.ai should emulate.
- **Linear "Built for the future" feature carousel** (https://linear.app) - 4-second auto-advance, dwell timing matches the recommended HQ.ai spec.

- Headline (Fraunces 40px, ink): `Just need one letter today? From $15.`

## Appendix A: What I deliberately did NOT recommend, and why

1. **No hero video.** Compliance-anxious buyers want to read at their own pace, not watch a brand film. A hero video plays once, costs 8-15s of attention, and 80% of B2B viewers leave before the message lands (per Wistia 2024 benchmarks `[MED]`). Use the chat-preview animation instead - it's interactive, on-message, and gives the buyer agency.

2. **No "Get notified" email capture on the marketplace CTA.** It converts at 1-3% (industry standard). "Reserve - first 100 get $10 off" converts at 4-8%. The discount cost is rounding-error compared to the email-list value at launch.

3. **No G2/Capterra/SOC2 badges in the trust strip.** The AU SMB buyer doesn't recognise G2, doesn't know what SOC2 means, and parses the badges as "expensive software". Trust comes from inline citations and a real founder face - not from compliance certificates the buyer can't decode.

4. **No "AI-powered" anywhere in the hero or above the fold.** Every product says it. The phrase is invisible. Lead with what the AI does (cites Fair Work, drafts letters) not the fact that it is one.

5. **No "Book a demo" CTA.** Self-serve is the wedge. Employment Hero requires a demo to even see pricing - that is the pain HQ.ai removes. Putting a demo CTA on the page would be brand-confusion. Founder Loom video is fine; demo booking is not.

6. **No multi-coloured gradient or hero background pattern.** Off-brand for the Ivory + Clay editorial direction. The page bg stays parchment-ivory throughout; the only colour is Clay, used sparingly on CTAs and accent states.

7. **No customer-logo cloud with 12+ unrecognisable logos.** 4-6 named, recognisable logos beat 20 unknown ones. If you don't have logos yet, replace with 5 industry icons (pub, trades, retail, allied health, professional services).

2. Companies with a "no credit card required" trial convert trial-signup-rate at 1.8x the rate of those requiring a card upfront, but their trial-to-paid rate is 35% LOWER. The net win is roughly equal - but the volume advantage matters more in the cold-traffic AU SMB segment where trust is the limiting factor.

Recommendation in light of this: stay with the no-card 14-day trial that HQ.ai already runs. Disclose all pricing on the landing page (already in the brief). Both moves are validated by the data and reinforce the wedge against Employment Hero's demo-gated model.
